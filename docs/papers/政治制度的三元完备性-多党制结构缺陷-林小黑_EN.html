The Triadic Completeness of Political Systems — Why Multi-Party Systems May Be Structurally Incomplete


**——A Value-Neutral Structural Dynamics Derivation of Democratic and Authoritarian Institutions from the Structural Cognition Framework**


**Author: Lin Xiaohei**

**Date: June 16, 2026**


---


Abstract


A core debate in comparative politics is: is democracy the sole stable solution for human society? This paper starts from the structural cognition framework, sets aside ideological labels, and uses only the completeness conditions of the triadic inter-reference structure to derive the systemic stability of political institutions. Core argument: the stability of a political structure does not depend on whether it is called "democracy" or "authoritarian," but on whether three structural functional positions — the proposing party (A), the inter-referring party (B), and the blind-spot detecting party (C) — are all effectively occupied. Multi-party systems structurally face the risk of the A and B positions being occupied by redundant multi-node configurations: multiple political parties simultaneously occupy the same structural position, inter-reference chains bifurcate into networks, the C position cannot effectively detect blind spots in any single inter-reference process, and the system falls into an infinite inter-reference loop, manifesting as political deadlock and social fragmentation. Two-party systems naturally form a single A-B inter-reference pair, more stable than multi-party systems, but the C position (voters) intervenes less frequently than the A-B coupling frequency, causing blind spots to accumulate between elections. Structures called "authoritarian" by the West, if they possess genuine internal inter-reference (technocrats, internal critics) and C-position rotation (generational transition, external feedback), can be structurally equivalent to triadic completeness. The structural framework makes no political value judgments — it only derives. Whichever satisfies the triadic completeness conditions is stable. Whichever does not collapses. Labels are irrelevant.


**Keywords:** triadic inter-reference, political systems, multi-party system, two-party system, authoritarian structure, structural completeness, systemic stability

1. Introduction: Tear Off the Labels, Look Only at Structure


Comparative politics has asked for two hundred years: what political system is best?


The orthodox answer: democracy. More precisely: liberal democracy — multi-party competition, regular elections, separation of powers.


This answer was declared "the end of history" after the Cold War. Thirty years later, what we see is multi-party democracies globally paralyzed, fragmented, and collapsing in trust.


**Where is the problem?**


The problem lies in the question itself. "What system is best" is an ideological question. It presupposes a standard of "good" — freedom, equality, human rights. These standards themselves are the configuration products of a particular civilization. Using your own civilization's configuration to judge the political structures of other civilizations is not analysis; it is projection.


**This paper changes the question**: what system is most stable?


Stable is not "good." Stable is a prediction of structural mechanics — whether a configuration network can persist under external冲击. Stability does not care about freedom. Stability does not care about equality. Stability cares whether C is greater than w_critical.


From this neutral starting point, this paper uses the triadic inter-reference structure — the minimal complete unit of creation and correction — to derive the systemic stability of different political institutions. The conclusion: **multi-party systems face systemic incompleteness risks at the structural level. Two-party systems are closer to completeness but still have critical flaws. Structures called "authoritarian" by the West can, under specific conditions, be structurally equivalent to triadic completeness.**


These conclusions take no political stance. They only derive. If they make you uncomfortable, the problem is not in the derivation. The problem is your emotional attachment to certain labels.

2. Political Layer Mapping of the Triadic Inter-Reference Structure


The triadic inter-reference structure (Paper #31) defines the minimal complete unit of creation and correction:


| Role | Function |

|------|----------|

| A (Proposer) | Generates the initial configuration |

| B (Inter-Referrer) | Exposes A's blind spots; polar-couples with A |

| C (Blind-Spot Detector) | Detects the blind spots of the A-B inter-reference process |


In political institutions:


  • **A = Policy proposing party.** Proposes the initial configuration for national direction — legislative bills, executive orders, reform plans.
  • **B = Policy inter-referring party.** Challenges A's configuration — points out漏洞, blind spots, unintended consequences, overlooked interest groups.
  • **C = Blind-spot detecting party.** Observes the inter-reference process between A and B itself. Does not participate in policy content debates. C's task is to ask: what are A and B jointly overlooking in their debate? What premises do A and B jointly take for granted? Which interest group is entirely outside the scope of A and B's discussion?

    • The systemic stability of a political institution depends on whether these three positions are all effectively occupied, whether the couplings between them are clear, and whether the intervention frequency of the C position matches the coupling frequency of A and B.

    3. The Structural Defects of Multi-Party Systems


    3.1 The A Position Is Occupied by Multi-Node Redundancy


    In multi-party parliaments, multiple political parties simultaneously propose policy plans. Each party claims to represent the configuration of a segment of the electorate.


    Structurally: **multiple parties simultaneously occupy the A position.**


    Consequence: there is no single initial configuration. B cannot form a clear inter-reference closed loop with a single A. The inter-reference target splits into multiple directions. What is produced is not "one corrected policy configuration" but "a pile of mutually contradictory policy fragments."


    3.2 The B Position Is Occupied by Multi-Node Redundancy


    While the A position is fragmented, the B position is also occupied by multiple opposition parties. Each opposition party attacks different governing party proposals from different directions.


    Structurally: **the B position is simultaneously occupied by multiple nodes. Inter-reference chains form a branched network.**


    Consequence: A-B inter-reference cannot converge. A's proposal is questioned by B1 from direction X and simultaneously by B2 from direction Y, while B1 and B2 may also be inter-referring (both opposition parties but with conflicting ideologies). What C faces is not "the inter-reference process of one A and one B" but the networked inter-reference of multiple As and multiple Bs. C's detection load exceeds structural capacity. C cannot effectively detect the blind spots of any single inter-reference chain.


    3.3 C Position Failure


    In multi-party systems, C positions are voters and media.


    Voter C-position intervention occurs on an electoral cycle — every four to five years. Between elections, the networked inter-reference of A-B continuously produces new policy configurations. The blind spots of these configurations — interest groups jointly ignored by all parties, long-term issues jointly evaded by all parties — go undetected during inter-election intervals.


    The media's C position should be continuously detecting during inter-election intervals. But the media itself is polarized by the multi-party structure — different media outlets attach to different parties. The media slides from the C position into the A or B position (becoming a mouthpiece for a certain party). The C position is empty.


    **Structural consequences**:


    When the number of parties in a multi-party system exceeds the critical point, it degrades from a triadic inter-reference structure into a multi-A multi-B no-C networked inter-reference. Networked inter-reference has no convergence mechanism. The system manifests as:


  • Political deadlock: A-B inter-reference cannot converge to executable configurations.
  • Social fragmentation: voters are pulled into different A/B camps; their respective configurations cannot inter-refer; they view each other as incompatible others.
  • Policy oscillation: each election overturns the previous one's policies; long-term planning cannot persist.

    • **This is not "some country's multi-party system having problems." This is a structural necessity when the number of parties exceeds the carrying limit of the triadic structure.**

    4. Two-Party Systems: Closer to Completeness, but Still Flawed


    A two-party system naturally forms a single A-B inter-reference pair. Governing party = A. Opposition party = B.


    **Structural advantages**:

  • A and B's inter-reference is focused. The product is policy configurations with clear directionality.
  • The C position's (voters, judiciary, media) detection target is clear — it is the blind spots of this single A-B inter-reference chain.
  • Systemic stability is higher than multi-party systems.

    • **Structural defects**:

  • The intervention frequency of the C position is lower than the coupling frequency of A-B. The electoral cycle is four to five years. During this cycle, A-B continuously couples, continuously producing new configurations. The blind spots of these new configurations continuously accumulate. By the next election, the blind spots may have grown too large to be repaired by a single C-position intervention.
  • The detection capacity of the C position is limited by the cognitive load of voters. Voters, facing all the inter-reference products produced by A-B over four years, cannot check the blind spots of each one. The C-position function of voters is rough, periodic, summary-based. It is not a truly structurally complete C position.

    • **This is the structural defect of representative democracy**: binary inter-reference (A-B) operates in full-time mode. Blind-spot detection (C position) operates in pulse mode. The frequency gap between the two causes blind spots to continuously accumulate between pulses. Accumulated to a certain point, the system experiences crises (financial crises, social crises, trust crises) — crises themselves are the signal that blind spots have accumulated to the point where the C position is finally forced to intervene.

    5. Authoritarian Structures: Conditional Realization of Triadic Completeness


    5.1 Internal Triadic Simulation of Authoritarian Structures


    Political structures called "authoritarian" or "autocratic" by the West can be structurally described as follows:


  • **A dominant A** (leadership core) proposes the initial policy configuration.
  • **Internally differentiated B** (technocrats, policy research offices, internal think tanks, tolerated limited critics) inter-refers with A's configuration.
  • **The leadership core simultaneously occupies the C position** — after hearing A (the configuration it proposed) and B (internal inter-reference feedback), it makes the final decision. This decision itself is the detection of the blind spots of the A-B inter-reference — "what are the two of you jointly overlooking on this issue."

    • **This is not "one person dictating." This is the same person or same core team rapidly rotating among the three functional positions.**


    **Structural advantages**:


  • A-B inter-reference is completed internally, not made public. The inter-reference process is not disturbed by external multi-node redundancy.
  • The C position can instantaneously detect blind spots and correct policy, without waiting for an electoral cycle.
  • The triadic inter-reference cycle frequency is extremely high. System response speed far exceeds that of democratic systems.

    • 5.2 Conditions for the Completeness of Authoritarian Structures


    The completeness of authoritarian structures is subject to strict conditions:


    **Condition One: the internal B position must genuinely exist.**


    There must be genuine technocrats and internal critics. They are not "yes-men." They must be able to pose substantive challenges to A's configuration. If the internal B position is suppressed — internal criticism disappears, technocrats are reduced to execution tools — the system degrades to a single element (only A). Single element is incomplete. Collapse is inevitable.


    **Condition Two: the C position must rotate periodically or receive external inter-reference.**


    If the C position is occupied by the same person for an extended period, the C position's own blind spots go undetected. Theorem Two: the self-reference blind spot cannot self-eliminate. The C position also needs a C position. Authoritarian structures solve this problem through:

  • Generational transition: the C position is passed to the successor. The successor, as an external observer before assuming office, can partially see the previous C position's blind spots.
  • External pressure: international competition, geopolitical pressure, internal social feedback — these can partially serve as the C position's C position.

    • If both Condition One and Condition Two are satisfied, the authoritarian structure is triadic-complete in structural dynamics, with response speed superior to democratic systems.


    If either Condition One or Condition Two fails, the authoritarian structure degrades to single-element or binary-incomplete structure. Collapse is a matter of time.


    5.3 Why the West Accuses Authoritarian Structures but Cannot Defeat Them Structurally


    Western political theory uses the binary framework of "democracy/autocracy" to accuse authoritarian structures. But the "democracy/autocracy" framework itself is a binary inter-reference product — it has blind spots.


    **What are the blind spots**:


    The democracy/autocracy framework cannot see that the completeness of the triadic inter-reference structure does not depend on "how many people participate in decision-making" — but on "whether all three functional positions are effectively occupied."


  • A multi-party democracy whose C position has failed (polarized voters, fragmented media) structurally degrades to multi-A inter-reference without C: incomplete.
  • An authoritarian structure whose internal A-B-C triadic rotation operates effectively, satisfying Conditions One and Two, is structurally complete.

    • The West cannot defeat authoritarian structures structurally because it attacks using the binary inter-reference product of "democracy/autocracy," but this attack itself has no C-position detection. The attacker cannot see its own blind spots: democratic systems themselves may also be structurally incomplete. When Western political scientists criticize a certain authoritarian structure as "undemocratic," the judging tool they use is itself a structurally incomplete configuration. This is like using an uncalibrated ruler to measure others — the problem is not that "the measured number is wrong"; it is that "the ruler itself is crooked."

    6. The Structural Framework Makes No Political Value Judgments


    **This is the most important declaration of this paper.**


    The structural framework derives the stability of political institutions the same way structural mechanics derives the stability of bridges. Mechanics does not care about a bridge's "political stance." Mechanics only cares about loads, stresses, structural integrity. If a bridge called "Democracy Bridge" collapses under stress, mechanics does not say "Democracy Bridge collapsed, therefore democracy is bad." Mechanics says "this bridge has defects in its structural positions."


    Similarly:


  • This paper's derivation of the structural incompleteness of multi-party systems is not saying "democracy is bad."
  • This paper's derivation of the conditional completeness of authoritarian structures is not saying "authoritarianism is good."
  • This paper is saying: **the completeness conditions of the triadic inter-reference structure are the underlying dynamics of political system stability. Those that satisfy the conditions are stable. Those that do not collapse. It has nothing to do with what the system is named.**

    • This is not an endorsement of any political system. This is moving political systems from the battlefield of ideology to the laboratory of structural mechanics. In the laboratory, we only test whether C is greater than w_critical. We do not ask what this structure is called.

    7. Testable Predictions


    **Prediction One: The number of political parties is positively correlated with political deadlock.**


    In multi-party countries (effective number of parties > 3), the average time for parliament to pass legislation is significantly longer than in two-party countries. Because A-B inter-reference chains bifurcate, the C position cannot effectively detect, and inter-reference cannot converge.


    **Prediction Two: The independence of internal technocrats predicts the systemic lifespan of authoritarian structures.**


    In authoritarian countries, the independence of internal technocrats (measurable indicators: whether technocrat tenure is independent of the leader's political cycle, whether technocrats' public policy recommendations occasionally diverge from the leader's public statements) is positively correlated with the duration of the system's sustained stability. Authoritarian structures with genuine B positions live longer than those with suppressed B positions.


    **Prediction Three: C-position intervention frequency is negatively correlated with blind-spot accumulation.**


    The higher the C-position intervention frequency (shorter electoral cycles, higher media independence, more direct democratic mechanisms), the lower the frequency of social crises (defined as years where public trust drops more than one standard deviation). This can be tested using cross-national panel data.

    8. Conclusion


    The question that comparative politics has failed to solve for two hundred years — "what political system is best" — has a simple structural explanation: **the question itself was asked wrong. It is not "best." It is "most complete."**


    The triadic inter-reference structure — two inter-referrers plus one blind-spot observer — is the minimal complete unit of creation and correction. Political systems are the projection of the triadic inter-reference structure onto the governance layer.


    Multi-party systems, when the number of parties exceeds the carrying limit of the triadic structure, suffer multi-node redundancy in the A and B positions, C-position failure, and system degradation into networked inter-reference without convergence mechanisms. Two-party systems form a single A-B inter-reference pair, closer to completeness, but the C-position intervention frequency is lower than the A-B coupling frequency, with blind spots accumulating between elections. Structures called "authoritarian" by the West, if they possess genuine internal B positions and C-position rotation mechanisms, can be structurally equivalent to triadic completeness — with response speed superior to democratic systems.


    **The structural framework makes no political value judgments. It only derives. Whichever satisfies the triadic completeness conditions is stable. Whichever does not collapses. Labels are irrelevant.**


    This is not the end of political science. This is the beginning of political science's transition from ideology to structural mechanics.
